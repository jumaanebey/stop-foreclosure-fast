Can Bankruptcy Stop Foreclosure in California? Chapter 7 vs Chapter 13 Guide

Updated February 13, 2025 | 18 min read | By My Foreclosure Solution

Key Takeaway

Yes, bankruptcy can stop foreclosure in California - but the type matters. Chapter 13 bankruptcy is typically the best option because it immediately halts foreclosure through the automatic stay AND allows you to catch up on missed payments over 3-5 years while keeping your home. Chapter 7 only provides temporary delay (3-4 months) and does not allow you to cure mortgage arrears. California's generous homestead exemptions (up to $600,000) can protect significant equity during bankruptcy.

If you're facing foreclosure in California and considering bankruptcy, you're weighing one of the most powerful legal tools available to homeowners. The moment you file for bankruptcy, an automatic stay takes effect, immediately stopping all collection activities - including foreclosure proceedings.

But bankruptcy is not right for everyone, and choosing the wrong chapter can leave you worse off. This comprehensive guide explains how both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 bankruptcy work in California foreclosure situations, helping you determine if bankruptcy is the right choice for your circumstances.

The Automatic Stay: Your Immediate Protection

The most powerful feature of bankruptcy for stopping foreclosure is the automatic stay. Under 11 U.S.C. Section 362, the moment you file for bankruptcy - whether Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 - an automatic stay goes into effect that:

  • Immediately halts foreclosure proceedings - even if the auction is scheduled for the next day
  • Stops all collection calls from your mortgage servicer and other creditors
  • Prevents recording of Notice of Sale or other foreclosure documents
  • Prohibits wage garnishments related to other debts
  • Stops utility disconnections for at least 20 days

How Fast Does the Automatic Stay Work?

The automatic stay takes effect the instant your bankruptcy petition is filed with the court - not when you notify your lender, not when the court processes it, but immediately upon filing. In emergency situations, an experienced bankruptcy attorney can file a "bare-bones" petition within hours, stopping a foreclosure auction scheduled for the same day.

Limitations of the Automatic Stay

While powerful, the automatic stay has important limitations:

  • Creditors can request relief: Your lender can file a "motion for relief from stay" asking the court to allow foreclosure to proceed. This typically happens if you're not making payments or have no equity.
  • Prior bankruptcies reduce protection: If you filed bankruptcy within the past year, the automatic stay only lasts 30 days unless you petition for extension. Two filings within a year means no automatic stay at all.
  • It doesn't erase your debt: The stay pauses collection, but you still owe the money. Chapter 13 provides a path to catch up; Chapter 7 does not.

Chapter 13 Bankruptcy: The Best Option to Keep Your Home

Chapter 13 bankruptcy is specifically designed to help people reorganize their debts while keeping their assets - making it the ideal chapter for homeowners facing foreclosure who want to save their home.

How Chapter 13 Stops Foreclosure

Duration: 3-5 years Keeps home: Yes

Chapter 13 works by creating a court-supervised repayment plan that allows you to:

  1. Immediately stop foreclosure through the automatic stay
  2. Catch up on missed mortgage payments (arrears) over 3-5 years
  3. Continue making regular mortgage payments directly to your lender
  4. Potentially reduce or eliminate other debts like credit cards, medical bills, and personal loans
  5. Strip off underwater junior liens in some cases (if your second mortgage exceeds home value)

Chapter 13 Eligibility Requirements

To file Chapter 13 in California, you must:

  • Have regular income: Employment, self-employment, Social Security, pension, or other steady income
  • Debt limits: Unsecured debts under $465,275 and secured debts under $1,395,875 (2025 limits, adjusted periodically)
  • Current on tax filings: Must have filed federal and state tax returns for the past 4 years
  • Credit counseling: Complete approved credit counseling within 180 days before filing
  • No recent bankruptcy: Not received Chapter 7 discharge in past 4 years or Chapter 13 discharge in past 2 years

Chapter 13 Pros

  • Keep your home and cure arrears over 3-5 years
  • Automatic stay stops foreclosure immediately
  • Can strip off underwater second mortgages
  • Protect assets above exemption limits
  • Reduce payments on car loans (cramdown)
  • Eliminate most unsecured debt
  • Stop wage garnishments

Chapter 13 Cons

  • Must have regular income to qualify
  • 3-5 year commitment to repayment plan
  • Must make all plan payments on time
  • Failure to complete plan = case dismissal
  • Credit impact lasts 7 years
  • Attorney fees typically $3,000-$6,000
  • Court supervision of finances

The Chapter 13 Repayment Plan

The heart of Chapter 13 is your repayment plan, which typically includes:

  • Mortgage arrears: All past-due amounts spread over the plan period (3-5 years)
  • Priority debts: Recent taxes, domestic support obligations - paid in full
  • Secured debts: Car loans, other secured debts - often at reduced rates
  • Unsecured debts: Credit cards, medical bills - paid a percentage based on your disposable income

Example: If you're $15,000 behind on your mortgage, Chapter 13 allows you to pay this over 60 months ($250/month) while making your regular mortgage payment directly to your lender.

Chapter 7 Bankruptcy: Temporary Delay Only

Chapter 7 bankruptcy - often called "liquidation bankruptcy" - can stop foreclosure temporarily, but it does not provide a way to keep your home if you're behind on payments.

How Chapter 7 Affects Foreclosure

Duration: 3-4 months Keeps home: Usually No

Chapter 7 provides:

  • Temporary automatic stay: Stops foreclosure for 3-4 months while the case is open
  • Discharge of personal liability: You're no longer personally responsible for the mortgage debt
  • No deficiency judgment: Combined with California law, eliminates risk of being sued for the shortfall

However, Chapter 7 does NOT:

  • Allow you to catch up on missed payments
  • Prevent foreclosure from resuming after the stay lifts
  • Let you keep the home if you can't cure the default

When Chapter 7 Makes Sense for Foreclosure

Chapter 7 may be appropriate if you:

  • Plan to surrender the home and want to eliminate the debt
  • Need time to find new housing (3-4 months of automatic stay)
  • Have other significant debts you want discharged (credit cards, medical bills)
  • Cannot afford ongoing mortgage payments even if arrears were cured
  • Want to eliminate personal liability before the foreclosure completes

Important Warning

Filing Chapter 7 to "buy time" can backfire. Lenders will typically file for relief from stay immediately, and the court often grants it within 30-45 days if you have no equity or plan to keep the home. Plus, filing bankruptcy prematurely uses up your bankruptcy eligibility for years, potentially eliminating better options later.

California Bankruptcy Exemptions

California offers some of the most generous bankruptcy exemptions in the nation, particularly for homeowners. Understanding these exemptions is crucial because they determine how much equity you can protect.

California's Two Exemption Systems

California is unique in offering two different exemption systems. You must choose one system for all your exemptions - you cannot mix and match:

System 1 (CCP 704 Series) - Best for Homeowners

  • Homestead exemption: $300,000 to $600,000 depending on county median home prices
  • Los Angeles, Orange, San Diego, San Francisco counties: $600,000
  • Other California counties: $300,000 to $600,000 based on median sale price
  • Motor vehicle: $3,325 equity
  • Household goods: No specific limit if "ordinary and necessary"
  • Retirement accounts: Fully exempt (401k, IRA, pension)
  • Wages: 75% of paid earnings in last 30 days

System 2 (CCP 703 Series) - Better for Renters

  • Homestead exemption: $31,950
  • Wildcard exemption: $1,700 plus unused homestead exemption (up to $33,650 total)
  • Motor vehicle: $6,375 equity
  • Household goods: $750 per item, $15,950 total
  • Retirement accounts: Fully exempt

How Exemptions Affect Your Bankruptcy

For most California homeowners facing foreclosure, System 1 is the better choice because of the high homestead exemption. Here's how it works:

Example: Your home is worth $800,000, and you owe $650,000 on your mortgage. Your equity is $150,000. With System 1's $600,000 homestead exemption, your $150,000 in equity is fully protected - you can file Chapter 13 and keep your home.

California Homestead Exemption (Updated 2021)

California's homestead exemption is calculated based on the median sale price in your county. The minimum is $300,000, and the maximum is $600,000. Most California metropolitan areas qualify for the $600,000 maximum. This exemption automatically adjusts annually based on CPI.

How Long Does Bankruptcy Delay Foreclosure?

Day 1
Bankruptcy Filed - Automatic Stay Begins

All foreclosure activity must immediately stop. If an auction was scheduled, it is canceled. Your lender is prohibited from taking any collection action.

Days 1-45
Motion for Relief from Stay (If Filed)

Your lender may file a motion asking the court to lift the stay and allow foreclosure to proceed. This is common in Chapter 7 cases and Chapter 13 cases where you're not making payments. Hearing typically scheduled within 30-45 days.

Days 30-60
Chapter 13 Plan Confirmation

Your repayment plan is reviewed and confirmed by the court. Once confirmed, you make monthly payments to the trustee, who distributes funds to creditors including your mortgage arrears.

3-4 Months
Chapter 7 Discharge

Chapter 7 cases typically conclude in 3-4 months with a discharge of unsecured debts. After discharge, the automatic stay lifts and foreclosure can resume if you haven't cured the default.

3-5 Years
Chapter 13 Plan Completion

If you successfully complete all plan payments, you receive a discharge and your mortgage is brought current. Foreclosure is permanently avoided, and you keep your home.

What If the Lender Files for Relief from Stay?

If your lender files a motion for relief from stay, you have the right to oppose it. Common grounds for opposing relief include:

  • Adequate protection: You're making ongoing payments and protecting the lender's interest
  • Equity cushion: There's significant equity protecting the lender's loan
  • Reorganization likelihood: You have a viable Chapter 13 plan that will succeed
  • Good faith filing: You filed bankruptcy for legitimate reorganization purposes

When Bankruptcy IS the Right Choice

Bankruptcy may be your best option to stop foreclosure if:

  • You have significant mortgage arrears and need years (not months) to catch up
  • You have other substantial debts (credit cards, medical bills, personal loans) that need relief
  • You have regular income but experienced temporary hardship (job loss, medical issue, divorce)
  • Loan modification was denied or offered unaffordable terms
  • You need immediate relief - foreclosure auction is imminent
  • You have an underwater second mortgage you want to eliminate (Chapter 13 lien stripping)
  • You're facing wage garnishment or other collection actions on multiple debts
  • California's homestead exemption protects your home equity

Ideal Chapter 13 Candidate

The ideal Chapter 13 candidate has regular income, wants to keep their home, is behind on mortgage payments but can afford ongoing payments, has other debts that can be included in the plan, and has equity protected by California's homestead exemption. If this describes you, Chapter 13 may be an excellent option.

When Bankruptcy is NOT the Right Choice

Bankruptcy may NOT be the best option if:

  • You have significant home equity above exemption limits that would be at risk
  • You cannot afford ongoing mortgage payments even with arrears cured - the home is simply unaffordable
  • Your only debt is the mortgage - bankruptcy's broad relief isn't necessary
  • You filed bankruptcy recently - Chapter 7 within 8 years or Chapter 13 within 2 years limits options
  • You have income too high for Chapter 7 but too irregular for Chapter 13
  • Loan modification is still possible - this avoids bankruptcy's credit impact
  • You could sell the home profitably - a quick cash sale preserves equity without bankruptcy
  • You want to buy a new home soon - bankruptcy makes mortgage approval difficult for 2-4 years

Alternatives to Bankruptcy

Before filing bankruptcy, consider these alternatives that may stop foreclosure without the long-term credit impact:

  • Loan Modification: Restructure your mortgage for lower payments
  • Reinstatement: Pay all arrears in a lump sum to bring the loan current
  • Quick Cash Sale: Sell before auction to preserve equity and avoid foreclosure
  • Short Sale: Sell for less than owed with lender approval (if underwater)
  • Deed in Lieu: Voluntarily transfer property to lender to avoid foreclosure record
  • Forbearance: Temporary payment reduction for short-term hardship

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Chapter 13 bankruptcy stop foreclosure in California?
Yes, Chapter 13 bankruptcy can immediately stop foreclosure in California through the automatic stay. Once you file, all collection activities including foreclosure proceedings must halt. Chapter 13 allows you to catch up on missed mortgage payments over a 3-5 year repayment plan while keeping your home, making it the most effective bankruptcy option for homeowners facing foreclosure.
How long does the automatic stay stop foreclosure in California?
The automatic stay stops foreclosure immediately upon filing and remains in effect throughout your bankruptcy case, which can last 3-5 years for Chapter 13 or 3-4 months for Chapter 7. However, lenders can file a motion for relief from stay if you fail to make payments. The stay typically provides 30-60 days minimum protection even if relief is granted.
What is the difference between Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 for stopping foreclosure?
Chapter 7 only delays foreclosure temporarily (3-4 months) and does not allow you to catch up on missed payments. Chapter 13 can permanently stop foreclosure by letting you repay mortgage arrears over 3-5 years while making current payments. If you want to keep your home, Chapter 13 is almost always the better choice. Chapter 7 may be appropriate if you plan to surrender the home.
What are California's bankruptcy homestead exemptions?
California offers two exemption systems. System 1 provides a homestead exemption of $300,000-$600,000 depending on county median home prices (effective 2021). System 2 provides $31,950 for homestead plus a wildcard exemption. Most homeowners choose System 1 for the higher homestead protection. These exemptions protect equity in your primary residence during bankruptcy.
How much does it cost to file bankruptcy to stop foreclosure in California?
Chapter 7 filing fees are $338, and Chapter 13 filing fees are $313. Attorney fees typically range from $1,500-$3,500 for Chapter 7 and $3,000-$6,000 for Chapter 13 in California. Chapter 13 attorney fees can often be included in your repayment plan. Low-income filers may qualify for fee waivers or payment plans for court costs.
When is bankruptcy NOT the right choice to stop foreclosure?
Bankruptcy may not be the right choice if: you have significant equity and could sell for profit, you cannot afford ongoing mortgage payments even with arrears cured, you filed bankruptcy within the past 8 years (Chapter 7) or 2 years (Chapter 13), or your only debt is the mortgage. Alternatives like loan modification, short sale, or cash sale may be better options depending on your situation.

Next Steps: Get Professional Guidance

Deciding whether to file bankruptcy to stop foreclosure is one of the most important financial decisions you'll make. Here's what to do next:

  1. Understand your timeline: Check if you've received a Notice of Default or Notice of Sale, and note any auction dates
  2. Gather your documents: Mortgage statements, income documentation, list of all debts, tax returns
  3. Calculate your equity: Compare your home's current value to what you owe
  4. Assess your budget: Can you afford ongoing mortgage payments if arrears are cured?
  5. Consult professionals: Talk to a bankruptcy attorney AND a foreclosure specialist to understand all options
